Foundations

What is Cybersecurity?

Understanding the principles protecting our digital lives — from personal devices to critical infrastructure.

Definition

Cybersecurity is the practice of protecting computers, networks, and data from malicious attacks, unauthorized access, damage, or theft. Also known as information technology (IT) security, it encompasses technologies, processes, and practices that safeguard digital systems across all sectors of modern society.

The field is built on three foundational principles — known as the CIA Triad — that guide how security is designed, evaluated, and maintained.

🔐

Confidentiality

Data is accessible only to those who are authorized.

✔️

Integrity

Data is accurate and has not been tampered with.

🔄

Availability

Authorized users can access data when they need it.

Attack Vectors

The Threat Landscape

Click any item to expand details and real-world examples.

🦠Malware
▾

Malware (malicious software) is any software intentionally designed to disrupt, damage, or gain unauthorized access to a system. Types include viruses, worms, trojans, adware, and spyware. It can steal, encrypt, or delete data and spy on user activity without consent.

Real Example: The WannaCry ransomware (2017) infected 200,000+ computers across 150 countries by exploiting a Windows vulnerability, crippling hospitals and banks.
🎣Phishing
▾

Phishing disguises attackers as trusted entities to trick victims into revealing passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data. These attacks occur via email, text message, or fake websites that closely mimic legitimate ones.

Real Example: In 2016, a phishing email tricked a US political campaign staffer into surrendering their Gmail credentials — the stolen emails were later published, influencing a major election.
💰Ransomware
▾

Ransomware encrypts a victim's files, making them inaccessible, then demands payment — usually in cryptocurrency — to restore access. Targets range from individuals to hospitals and government agencies. Even after paying, file recovery is not guaranteed.

Real Example: The 2021 Colonial Pipeline attack shut down the largest US fuel pipeline, causing East Coast fuel shortages. The company paid $4.4 million in ransom.
🕵️Spyware
▾

Spyware secretly monitors user activity without consent, recording keystrokes, capturing screenshots, tracking browsing, and harvesting passwords and financial information. It often arrives bundled with seemingly legitimate free software.

Real Example: Pegasus spyware, developed by NSO Group, infected smartphones silently with no user interaction and was reportedly used to surveil journalists and activists globally.
🤝Social Engineering
▾

Social engineering manipulates people psychologically — exploiting trust, fear, greed, or urgency — to gain unauthorized access or information. Unlike technical attacks, social engineering targets human behavior. Common forms include pretexting, baiting, and impersonation.

Real Example: In 2020, Twitter employees were called by attackers impersonating IT staff. The attackers gained internal tool access and hijacked major accounts to run a Bitcoin scam.
🌊DDoS Attacks
▾

A Distributed Denial of Service attack floods a server with traffic from thousands of compromised devices (a botnet), making it unavailable to legitimate users. These attacks can take down websites, online services, and even entire ISPs.

Real Example: The 2016 Mirai botnet used hundreds of thousands of hacked IoT devices to knock Netflix, Twitter, and Reddit offline for hours.
🔑Password Attacks
▾

Password attacks attempt to gain unauthorized access by cracking or stealing credentials. Methods include brute force (trying all combinations), dictionary attacks, and credential stuffing (using previously leaked password lists). Weak or reused passwords are the primary vulnerability.

Real Example: The 2012 LinkedIn breach exposed 117 million credential pairs. Attackers used these in credential stuffing campaigns on other sites for years afterward.

Take Action

How to Protect Yourself

Practical habits you can start implementing right now to dramatically reduce your risk online.

🔐

Strong Passwords

Your first and most critical line of defense against unauthorized access.

  • Use at least 12–16 characters
  • Mix uppercase, lowercase, numbers & symbols
  • Never reuse passwords across different sites
  • Use a password manager (Bitwarden, 1Password)
📱

Two-Factor Authentication

2FA ensures a stolen password alone can't unlock your account.

  • Enable on email, banking, and social media
  • Use an authenticator app (Authy, Google Auth)
  • Prefer app-based 2FA over SMS when possible
  • Back up your recovery codes securely
🔗

Avoid Suspicious Links

Phishing links are crafted to appear legitimate — always verify first.

  • Hover over links to preview the actual URL
  • Watch for misspellings (paypa1.com vs paypal.com)
  • Don't click links in unexpected emails or texts
  • Use VirusTotal to scan suspicious URLs
🔄

Keep Software Updated

Updates patch security vulnerabilities before attackers can exploit them.

  • Enable automatic updates on all devices
  • Prioritize OS and browser updates
  • Update router firmware regularly
  • Never ignore update notifications
🛡️

Use Antivirus Software

Antivirus detects and removes malware before it can cause damage.

  • Install reputable software (Malwarebytes, Bitdefender)
  • Keep virus definitions updated automatically
  • Enable real-time protection
  • Run scheduled full-system scans
📶

Secure Wi-Fi Practices

Public and poorly configured Wi-Fi is a prime target for interception.

  • Use WPA3 encryption on your home router
  • Change default router admin credentials
  • Use a VPN on public networks
  • Avoid sensitive tasks on public Wi-Fi

⚡ Quick Win — Do This Today

Enable two-factor authentication on your email account right now. Your email is the master key to all your other accounts — if it's compromised, everything reachable from it is at risk. Takes 5 minutes and dramatically increases your security.

Long-Term Habits

Cybersecurity Best Practices

Build security-first habits that protect you every day.

Browsing

Safe Browsing Habits

🔒

Always Check for HTTPS

Before entering any personal information, ensure the website uses HTTPS. HTTP sites transmit data in plaintext readable by anyone on the same network.

🧩

Manage Browser Extensions

Only install extensions from trusted sources. Malicious extensions can silently read everything you type — including passwords and card numbers.

🍪

Cookies & Privacy Settings

Regularly clear cookies and browsing data. Use private mode for sensitive sessions. Consider a privacy-focused browser like Firefox or Brave.

Personal Data

Protecting Your Information

📋

Minimize Data Sharing

Only provide personal information when truly necessary. Read privacy policies. Opt out of data collection wherever possible — less shared means less that can be stolen.

📱

Review App Permissions

Regularly audit which apps have access to your camera, microphone, location, and contacts. Revoke anything that doesn't match the app's purpose.

🌐

Social Media Privacy

Set accounts to private. Never post home addresses, vacation plans, or financial information publicly. Attackers use public posts for targeting and social engineering.

🗑️

Digital Cleanup

Delete accounts you no longer use. Old accounts with outdated passwords are easy targets. Check Have I Been Pwned to see if your data has been exposed.

Email Safety

Recognizing Scam Emails

📧Urgency & Pressure Tactics
▾

Scam emails create false urgency: "Your account will be closed in 24 hours!" This is designed to bypass your critical thinking. Legitimate organizations never demand immediate action of this nature via email.

📧Suspicious Sender Addresses
▾

Always check the full sender address, not just the display name. Scammers use addresses like "[email protected]" or "[email protected]". Real companies only send from their official domains.

📧Requests for Personal Data
▾

Legitimate banks and organizations will NEVER ask for your password, SSN, or full card number via email. Treat any such request as a scam and contact the organization through their official website directly.

📧Poor Grammar & Formatting
▾

Many phishing emails contain grammatical errors, unusual phrasing, or awkward formatting. Legitimate companies proofread their communications. Errors are a reliable red flag — especially combined with other warning signs.

Data Safety

Backup Strategies

The 3-2-1 Backup Rule

3

Copies of your data

2

Different storage types

1

Offsite or cloud copy

Example: your original files on a computer (1), a backup on an external drive (2), and a cloud backup via Google Drive or Backblaze (3 — offsite). This protects against ransomware, hardware failure, and physical disasters.
